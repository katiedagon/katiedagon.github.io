Land-Atmosphere Coupling
As a climate scientist, I am interested in many aspects of the Earth system and how they evolve over time. My particular focus involves using models to investigate changes in land-atmosphere coupling, surface energy balance, and the hydrologic cycle. Greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide (CO2) are important drivers of climate change. CO2 exerts a strong physiological effect through plant photosynthesis and water use, as well as its warming effect. My interests lie in exploring the feedbacks on climate through the impact of CO2 on plants; including soil moisture, temperature variability, and extreme events such as heat waves and drought.

A variety of solar geoengineering methods.		 Solar Geoengineering
We are already on our way to a much warmer world. As humans continue to emit greenhouse gases into the atmosphere, avoiding the worst increases in surface temperature will likely require climate mitigation or even climate intervention. Solar geoengineering refers to a set of proposed methods to mitigate climate changes from greenhouse warming by manipulating the incoming solar radiation. To fully understand the consequences of solar geoengineering, research is needed to examine the impacts it will have on the climate system and understand how those impacts compare to the climate changes induced by a warming world. My research explores the climate impacts of solar geoengineering in a modeling context, focusing in particular on land-atmosphere coupling, terrestrial water cycling, regional climate variability, and vegetation-climate interactions (e.g., Dagon and Schrag, 2016).
Climate Modeling
My primary research tool is climate modeling, in particular the Community Earth System Model (CESM). I focus on modeling terrestrial systems, and have utilized the Community Land Model (CLM) and Community Atmosphere Model (CAM) to investigate changes in land-atmosphere coupling resulting from climate change and solar geoengineering.
