Land-Atmosphere Coupling
I am interested in using models to investigate changes in land-atmosphere coupling, surface energy balance, and the hydrologic cycle resulting from climate changes such as increasing concentrations of greenhouse gases like carbon dioxide. CO2 exerts a strong physiological effect through plant photosynthesis and water use, as well as its radiative effect. My interests lie in exploring the feedbacks on climate through the impact of CO2 on plants; including soil moisture, temperature variability, and extreme events such as heat waves and drought.

A variety of solar geoengineering methods.		 Solar Geoengineering
I study the climate impacts of solar geoengineering, a set of methods to mitigate global warming by reducing the amount of incoming solar radiation to the Earth's surface. While these methods have the potential to avoid some of the temperature increases due to global warming, not enough is known about their potential side effects. I have used observations and models of varying complexity to study how solar geoengineering could induce unintended changes in climate (e.g., Dagon and Schrag, 2016).
Climate Modeling
My primary research tool is climate modeling, in particular the Community Earth System Model (CESM). I focus on land surface models, and have utilized the Community Land Model (CLM) and Community Atmosphere Model (CAM) to investigate changes in land-atmosphere coupling resulting from climate change and solar geoengineering.
