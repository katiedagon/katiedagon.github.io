Land Surface Modeling
I use land surface models, specifically the NCAR Community Land Model (CLM) to investigate changes in land-atmosphere coupling, surface energy balance, and the hydrologic cycle resulting from increasing concentrations of greenhouse gases such as carbon dioxide. CO2 exerts a strong physiological effect through plant photosynthesis and water use, as well as its radiative effect. My interests lie in exploring the feedbacks on climate through the impact of CO2 on plants; including soil moisture, temperature variability, and extreme events such as heat waves and drought.

An application of my research considers geoengineering by solar radiation management (SRM), a set of methods to mitigate global warming by reducing the amount of incoming solar radiation to the Earth's surface. While these methods, such as stratospheric sulfate aerosol injection, have the potential to avoid some of the temperature increases due to global warming, not enough is known about their potential side effects. I have used observations and models of varying complexity to study how SRM could induce unintended changes in climate (e.g. Dagon and Schrag, 2016).
